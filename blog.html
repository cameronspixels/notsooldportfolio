Building the Site

As I’d imagine most students might go, previous iterations of my portfolio send shudders down my spine. I felt that poor design and development decisions couldn’t just be a lack of knowledge though, and a lot of people in industry put empathises on planning - and you could include in that wireframing, prototyping, sticking post-it notes everywhere… which is where I ended up when it came to defining layout.

If there’s something that I’ve come to notice since starting working with the web, it’s that design is far from my strong point. I can sit in a code editor and work for days on end, but give me a colour palette and I’ll need a week figuring that out alone.

Wary of this, I kept my design decisions simple. Black on white, accented just by red, and various weights of the font Raleway throughout. In theory, even I’d be able to manage that.

This is one of the parts of design which I’ve constantly tried to pay attention to. Not doing so is essentially a method of exclusion - and though we can’t all apply that feeling to websites, we’d all know what it’s like for something to be, by design, restricting. Think food allergies, for example - nobody wants to be forced to go hungry.

As such, I’ve made an attempt to implement appropriate features from the ground-up. Alt tags and titles are of course a go-to, but I also applied along the way things that I didn’t originally think about. It wasn’t until I attended a talk at Wordcamp Manchester 2017, for example, that I realised how poor browser default focusing way - if forced to use just a keyboard, you’d sometimes not even know that you were tabbing through a page.
